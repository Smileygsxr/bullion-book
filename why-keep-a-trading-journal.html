Why Keep a Trading Journal?

Bullion Book Guides · Updated July 2026

Ask a trader how last month went and you'll get a story. Ask their journal and you'll get the truth. The gap between those two answers is where most trading accounts quietly die - and closing that gap is the entire point of journaling.

Your memory is the least reliable tool you trade with

Human memory doesn't store trades - it stores feelings about trades. The revenge trade that got back to breakeven files itself away as "disciplined". The oversized winner becomes proof of skill rather than luck. Psychologists call this outcome bias, and every trader has it. A journal is the only defense: it records what you actually did, at the moment you did it, before the result had a chance to edit the story.

Five things a journal does that nothing else can

1. It shows you your real edge - or that you don't have one yet

Win rate, average win vs average loss, expectancy per trade, profit factor: these numbers only exist if the underlying trades are recorded. After 30-50 logged trades you stop arguing with yourself about whether a setup "works" - you look it up. Many traders discover that one setup carries their whole account while another quietly drains it, something no amount of screen time reveals.

2. It ends repeat mistakes

Most losing trades aren't new mistakes - they're reruns. Moving a stop, revenge-trading after a loss, oversizing on "sure things". When each trade carries a note about why you took it, patterns surface within weeks. You can't fix a habit you can't see; a journal makes it visible.

3. It forces honest risk numbers

Logging your stop-loss with every entry turns risk into a measurable unit - the R-multiple. A +2R trade and a +0.3R trade might make the same dollars on different position sizes, but only one of them is a good trade. Thinking in R is the single fastest upgrade to how most retail traders evaluate themselves, and it starts with writing the stop down.

4. It builds discipline mechanically, not through willpower

Knowing you'll have to write "entered without a setup, bored" changes behavior before the trade happens. The journal becomes a pre-trade filter: if you'd be embarrassed to log it, don't take it. Traders who journal consistently take fewer, better trades - not because they became more disciplined overnight, but because the act of recording adds friction exactly where friction helps.

5. It turns your weekly review into evidence, not vibes

A weekly review with a journal answers concrete questions: which day of the week hurt you, which hour, which pair, what your average hold time was on winners vs losers. Without the data, a "review" is just re-experiencing the week's emotions with a coffee.

What to log on every trade

The minimum that makes the analysis above possible:

  • The facts: date and time, symbol, direction, entry price, exit price, position size, fees.
  • The plan: stop-loss and target at entry - this is what makes R-multiples and risk/reward measurable.
  • The setup: a tag or playbook name ("breakout", "news fade") so trades group into comparable buckets.
  • The state: one honest line about why you entered and how you felt. This is the line future-you learns the most from.

Paper, spreadsheet, or app?

Paper builds the habit but can't compute anything. A spreadsheet computes, but every stat is a formula you maintain by hand, and charts, screenshots and psychology notes never quite fit. A dedicated journal app does the recording and the analysis: log or import the trade, and win rate, expectancy, R-multiples, calendars and day/hour breakdowns update themselves. The best choice is whichever one you'll still be using in six months - which, in practice, is usually the one that costs the least effort per trade.
